## Author Note

This paper presents the concept of **CIVITAS Public** as an initial public-facing and trust-building layer of the broader CIVITAS vision within the ERDA framework. It is intended as a strategic and practical project paper for further discussion, refinement, prototyping, institutional dialogue, and possible publication in an open-access repository.

---

## Abstract

This concept paper outlines **CIVITAS Public** as a first public entry layer and trust architecture for a future European digital agora within the ERDA context. The project is designed as a democratically oriented, non-commercial, publicly trustworthy and technologically sovereign space for European civic publication, structured debate, trend observation and gradual democratic participation.

CIVITAS Public is based on four core principles: first, open readability for the broader public; second, trustworthy publication through verification and accountability; third, structured democratic debate instead of outrage-optimised platform dynamics; and fourth, an expandable institutional and technical architecture that can later evolve into a fuller CIVITAS environment.

The paper defines the problem context, normative purpose, project scope, governance model, role system, publication logic, review layers, polling and trend functions, legal and procedural safeguards, technical architecture, roadmap, risk profile, and strategic value of the initiative. It argues that CIVITAS Public can serve as a realistic and visible first step towards a European civic digital infrastructure that is neither captured by advertising logic nor reduced to bureaucratic state communication.

**Keywords:** CIVITAS; ERDA; digital democracy; European public sphere; civic technology; democratic infrastructure; public debate; verification; Europe; open source

## 1. Introduction

Europe possesses a rich democratic heritage, yet its digital public sphere is structurally dependent on privately governed platforms optimised primarily for reach, emotional reaction, behavioural capture and commercial monetisation. These systems may provide scale, but they do not reliably provide democratic quality, accountable visibility, or durable public orientation.

At the same time, there is a growing need for a European digital space that can combine civic publication, qualified discourse, thematic continuity, accessible political explanation, and structured citizen participation. Such a space must not become a mere extension of state broadcasting, nor should it replicate the chaotic incentives of mass social media. It must remain open enough to sustain public life and sufficiently structured to uphold trust, accountability, and democratic usefulness.

**CIVITAS Public** is proposed as a practical response to this need. It is not conceived as the entirety of CIVITAS, but as a first operational layer: visible, useful, institutionally extensible, and capable of demonstrating that a European digital agora can begin in a realistic and credible form.

## 2. Problem Statement

The current European digital communication environment suffers from several structural deficits.

### 2.1 Commercial platform dominance

A considerable share of public discourse is shaped by systems whose primary incentives are advertising, retention, amplification and behavioural profiling rather than democratic clarity or civic responsibility.

### 2.2 Erosion of trust and orientation

Users often struggle to distinguish between well-grounded analysis, ideological messaging, low-quality commentary, coordinated manipulation and superficial reaction content.

### 2.3 Weak institutional continuity

Thoughtful civic input frequently disappears into unstructured feeds instead of developing into dossiers, deliberative sequences, public memory or institutionally relevant follow-up.

### 2.4 Fragmented European discourse

Debates remain split across national, linguistic and platform boundaries, often without adequate translation, contextual bridging or durable European thematic structure.

### 2.5 Low-threshold publication without layered quality signals

Openness is valuable, but without differentiated role systems, review logic and transparent responsibility structures, openness can turn into opacity and noise.

Taken together, these deficits are not only communicative problems. They are democratic infrastructure problems.

## 3. Project Purpose and Scope

CIVITAS Public is meant to serve as an initial European civic publication and debate platform with the following core operating logic:

> **Readable for all. Trustworthy through verification. Valuable through accountability.**

Its scope is deliberately limited and focused. It is not meant to replace parliaments, the press, academia, civil society organisations or civic institutions. Instead, it is meant to provide a structured digital layer between them: one that makes public contribution more trustworthy, more comparable, more durable and more accessible.

CIVITAS Public combines the following functions:

- a publication platform,
- a structured debate space,
- a polling and trend layer,
- a thematic dossier system,
- an expandable trust architecture for future democratic participation.

Bürgerbeteiligte Aufsicht, Ethikkomitees für algorithmische Entscheidungen, öffentliche Audits sowie der Verzicht auf Werbung und kommerzielle Datenverwertung sollen Vertrauen und demokratische Legitimation sichern.

Technisch ist CIVITAS als dezentrale, Open-Source- und Fediverse-kompatible Plattform vorgesehen, mit europäischem Hosting, DSGVO-, eIDAS- und EMRK-Konformität, Mehrsprachigkeit, Barrierefreiheit und transparenten Algorithmen ohne versteckte Rankings. Zu den Kernfunktionen gehören ein verifizierter Debattenraum mit KI- und menschlicher Moderation, Bürgeranträge, Schnittstellen zu Wahlen und Konsultationen, politische Informationsmodule wie Abstimmungstracker sowie Civic-Tech-Werkzeuge zur Mitgestaltung.

Zugleich setzt das Konzept auf Rechtssicherheit und Missbrauchsschutz: rechtsstaatliche Moderationsregeln, unabhängige Widerspruchsverfahren, Transparenzberichte, externe Audits und Schutz vor koordinierter Einflussnahme oder Überlastung. Ergänzt wird dies durch Partnerschaften mit Parlamenten, Kommunen, Bildungseinrichtungen, Medienhäusern und demokratischen Partnerstaaten sowie durch besondere Zugänge für Schulen, Hochschulen und Jugendräte. So versteht sich CIVITAS nicht nur als technisches Projekt, sondern als strategischer Pfeiler europäischer Demokratie.

## 5. CIVITAS Declaration (British English)

**CIVITAS** is envisioned as Europe’s digital agora: a democratically governed, citizen-oriented and technologically sovereign public space. It is not meant to replicate a commercial social media platform, but to serve as a trustworthy civic infrastructure for participation, freedom of expression and democratic agency. Its purpose is to strengthen citizen involvement, make political processes more transparent, and foster democratic culture over the long term.

The platform is intended to be run by an independent European foundation under parliamentary oversight, with civil society organisations, youth councils, media and academia involved in its governance. Public-interest funding, citizen participation in oversight bodies, ethics committees for algorithmic decisions, and public audits are meant to ensure legitimacy, transparency and trust, while excluding advertising and commercial data exploitation.

From a technical perspective, CIVITAS is designed as a decentralised, open-source, fediverse-compatible platform, hosted in Europe and compliant with GDPR, eIDAS and the European Convention on Human Rights. It is meant to be multilingual, accessible, modular and transparent in its algorithmic logic, with no hidden rankings. Its core functions include a verified debate space moderated by both AI and humans, citizens’ petitions, interfaces to elections and public consultations, political information modules such as voting trackers, and civic-tech tools for shaping public initiatives.

At the same time, the concept places strong emphasis on rule-of-law safeguards and resilience: clear moderation rules, independent appeal procedures, transparency reports, external audits, and protection against coordinated manipulation or system overload. CIVITAS is also intended to work with parliaments, municipalities, educational institutions, media organisations and democratic partner countries, while offering safe participation channels for schools, universities and youth councils. In this sense, it is presented not merely as a technical platform, but as a strategic pillar of Europe’s democratic future.

## 6. Operational Project Model

The concrete operational purpose of CIVITAS Public is the following:

> To establish a European public publishing and debate platform where all may read, while publishing rights are initially limited to verified and certified actors; supported by transparent polling, trend reporting, citizen questions, and structured thematic dossiers.

This leads to four immediate operational goals.

### 6.1 Trustworthy publication

Public contributions should not appear as anonymous fragments within opaque feeds, but as accountable publications with clear authorship, role attribution, source logic and revision history.

### 6.2 Structured public orientation

Instead of endless reaction chains, the platform should privilege thematic rooms, dossiers, question formats, debate structures and evolving issue maps.

### 6.3 European intelligibility

The system should be multilingual, transnationally legible and capable of connecting national public conversations without erasing their contextual specificity.

### 6.4 Expandable institutional architecture

The initial system should be designed in such a way that later modules, such as petitions, consultations, educational interfaces, local civic rooms or parliamentary connectors, can be added without redesigning the entire platform logic.

## 7. Role System

A layered role structure is essential if openness and quality are to be combined.

### 7.1 Readers

All people may access, read, search, cite and share public content.

### 7.2 Registered Participants

Registered users may take part in polls, follow topics, submit questions, react within defined formats, and contribute to certain participation layers.

### 7.3 Verified Authors

These users have a confirmed identity and may publish under accountable profiles. Verification is a trust mechanism, not a mechanism of social exclusion.

### 7.4 Certified Publishers

These actors carry an additional layer of editorial, professional, institutional or civic qualification. Possible categories include:

- journalistic review status,
- research or expert review status,
- civic or municipal practice status,
- civil society legitimacy,
- youth representation status,
- invited and reviewed guest contribution status,
- civic-tech expertise status.

This layered model allows the platform to remain publicly accessible while ensuring that publication carries responsibility and interpretive clarity.

## 8. Publication Model

### 8.1 Core publication formats

The platform should begin with a limited number of clearly defined formats:

1. **Article** – a structured publication with sources, author profile, topic assignment and version history.  
2. **Brief** – a shorter, highly focused analytical or explanatory piece.  
3. **Dossier** – a thematic collection of developments, voices, sources and issue trajectories.  
4. **Citizen Question** – a public civic question with a qualified response.  
5. **Debate Format** – a structured multi-perspective exchange.  
6. **Trend Report** – an interpreted summary of issue dynamics and platform resonance.

### 8.2 Persistent citation logic

At an early stage, full DOI registration is not yet necessary. Instead, the project should begin with an internal, stable and citable **CIVITAS-ID** system including:

- unique identifier,
- publication date,
- version number,
- author profile,
- thematic room,
- stable URL,
- recommended citation string.

Example:

`CIVITAS-PUBLIC-2026-ARTICLE-00017-v1`

A DOI-based layer may later be introduced for selected, higher-value or archival publication formats.

### 8.3 Versioning

Every publication should include visible revision history so that updates, corrections and expansions remain publicly intelligible.

## 9. Review and Quality Assurance

A full academic peer review model for all content would be too heavy for the initial phase. A layered and transparent model is more appropriate.

### 9.1 Level A – Basic review

Mandatory for all publications:

- identity or role verification,
- formal compliance,
- source and format check,
- rule conformity,
- content type labelling.

### 9.2 Level B – Editorial second look

For more substantial pieces:

- plausibility review,
- structure and clarity review,
- source logic review,
- format refinement.

### 9.3 Level C – Expert or open review

For major analytical, conceptual or methodological texts:

- optional expert second review,
- documented review notes,
- possible publication of review state or review comments.

### 9.4 Public transparency

Each published item should visibly show:

- its format,
- its review status,
- its correction status,
- the responsible contributor or editorial entity.

## 10. Polling, Trends and Public Resonance

Polling and trend observation may become attractive entry points, but only if their meaning is carefully distinguished.

### 10.1 Poll types

1. **Community Poll** – a fast platform-internal mood indicator.  
2. **Structured Poll** – a more carefully framed issue-specific poll.  
3. **Expert Snapshot** – a qualified perspective from a narrower professional or civic group.  
4. **Youth Pulse** – targeted youth participation format.

### 10.2 Trend layers

Trend reporting may focus on:

- issue ascent,
- sustained debate clusters,
- conflict lines,
- density of civic proposals,
- cross-linguistic resonance patterns.

### 10.3 Transparency rule

The platform must always make explicit whether a poll or trend result is:

- platform-internal,
- curated and interpretive,
- more methodologically grounded,
- or merely indicative.

CIVITAS Public should never simulate representativeness where none exists.

## 11. Attractive Initial Modules

For early relevance and public visibility, several attractive but serious modules should be launched.

### 11.1 Europe in Brief

Short, clear and citable explanatory texts on current European topics.

### 11.2 Trend Radar Europe

Regular summaries of which questions and issue clusters are gaining traction.

### 11.3 Debate Dossiers

One issue, several perspectives, sources, tensions and possible pathways forward.

### 11.4 Citizen Questions

Public questions submitted by citizens and answered by verified authors, editors or experts.

### 11.5 Policy Tracker

Structured tracking of public policy development: announced, negotiated, adopted, blocked or implemented.

### 11.6 Young CIVITAS

A dedicated participation and learning area for schools, youth councils, students and young European voices.

### 11.7 CIVITAS Dialogues

Moderated conversation formats with thematic boundaries, documented process and quality moderation.

## 12. Governance Framework

CIVITAS Public can only become trustworthy if its governance is visibly well designed from the beginning.

### 12.1 Guiding principles

- no advertising,
- no commercial data exploitation,
- public rules,
- procedural fairness,
- appeal mechanisms,
- transparency reports,
- democratic orientation,
- anti-capture safeguards.

### 12.2 Suggested governance structure

**a) Foundational carrier structure**  
Responsible for legal form, funding integrity, institutional continuity and strategic independence.

**b) Editorial and Standards Board**  
Responsible for publication rules, formats, review logic and quality standards.

**c) Civic Oversight Panel**  
A citizen-facing oversight body concerned with fairness, intelligibility and legitimacy.

**d) Ethics and Algorithm Review Group**  
Responsible for the evaluation of moderation systems, ranking logic, AI assistance and systemic risk.

**e) Appeals and Due Process Unit**  
Independent review body for complaints, labelling disputes, access conflicts and moderation challenges.

## 13. Technical Architecture

### 13.1 Core design principles

CIVITAS Public should be built as a modular, transparent and interoperable system rather than a closed black-box platform.

### 13.2 Technical objectives

- open source,
- European hosting,
- multilingual support,
- accessibility by design,
- GDPR-compliant data handling,
- clear rights and roles,
- exportable content,
- versioned publication logic,
- extensibility,
- future federation capability.

### 13.3 Likely system layers

1. **Publication Layer** – articles, briefs, dossiers and questions.  
2. **Identity and Role Layer** – registration, verification, certification and permissions.  
3. **Resonance Layer** – polls, trend logic and interaction signals.  
4. **Moderation and Review Layer** – human moderation, AI support, review status, appeals.  
5. **Federation and API Layer** – future interoperability and system-level extensibility.

## 14. Legal Safeguards and Procedural Protection

Because CIVITAS Public structures political and civic visibility, rule-of-law safeguards are not peripheral. They are foundational.

### 14.1 Required elements

- clearly defined publication and participation rules,
- visible labelling rules,
- documented moderation decisions,
- graduated enforcement logic,
- independent complaint channels,
- anti-bot and anti-manipulation safeguards,
- overload protection,
- privacy and security protections.

### 14.2 Moderation principle

The aim should not be maximum noise or maximum passivity, but maximum democratic usability. Moderation must be rule-bound, explainable and reviewable.

## 15. Roadmap

### Phase 0 – Concept and network formation

- finalise concept paper,
- define narrative and public purpose,
- prepare minimal website concept,
- open first strategic conversations with relevant actors,
- define role and verification scheme.

### Phase 1 – MVP

- launch articles, briefs and citizen questions,
- implement CIVITAS-ID system,
- create verified author profiles,
- deploy simple polling,
- publish initial dossiers,
- publish foundational rules and standards.

### Phase 2 – Trust and depth expansion

- make review layers visible,
- introduce trend reports,
- launch debate and youth modules,
- formalise appeal process,
- strengthen editorial and civic partnerships.

### Phase 3 – Institutional extension

- assess DOI integration,
- build broader civic-tech interfaces,
- strengthen municipal and parliamentary connections,
- deepen multilingual operations,
- expand interoperability and federation logic.

## 16. Strategic Value

CIVITAS Public offers value across several stakeholder groups.

### 16.1 Citizens

- better orientation,
- more trustworthy information,
- structured participation,
- less algorithmic chaos.

### 16.2 Media-adjacent actors

- visible space for accountable contribution,
- publication layer beyond platform logic,
- improved European discoverability.

### 16.3 Academic and expert communities

- public-facing publication channel,
- thematic dossier logic,
- knowledge transfer into wider society.

### 16.4 Youth and education

- safer and more meaningful participation spaces,
- democratic learning through real public formats,
- entry paths into European civic discourse.

### 16.5 Institutions and civil society

- structured issue mapping,
- publicly visible civic questions,
- better continuity between debate and follow-up.

## 17. Risks and Mitigation

### 17.1 Risk: perception of elitism

If publication is limited to verified and certified actors, the project may be perceived as exclusive.

**Mitigation:**  
clear access pathways, open citizen question formats, reviewed guest contributions, transparent participation architecture.

### 17.2 Risk: insufficient early momentum

Without visible usefulness, the project may remain abstract.

**Mitigation:**  
early deployment of attractive high-utility formats such as Europe in Brief, Trend Radar, Citizen Questions and Debate Dossiers.

### 17.3 Risk: governance overload

Too much complexity at the outset may paralyse progress.

**Mitigation:**  
start with a minimal but principled governance core and scale it step by step.

### 17.4 Risk: confusion with ordinary social media

If presentation and communication are poorly designed, the project may be mistaken for yet another feed platform.

**Mitigation:**  
clear format logic, thematic structure, visible standards and deliberate distance from conventional feed incentives.

## 18. Discussion

CIVITAS Public is not a utopian replacement system. Its value lies precisely in its intermediate function. It aims to occupy the currently underbuilt space between open discourse and trustworthy structure, between digital reach and democratic maturity, between public visibility and responsible continuity.

Restricting publication initially to verified and certified actors is not proposed as an end in itself, but as a practical starting mechanism for trust formation. However, this restriction can only remain legitimate if accompanied by real civic accessibility, transparent review procedures, open input channels and visible opportunities for future expansion.

If successful, CIVITAS Public would not simply add another platform to Europe’s digital landscape. It could help establish a different public habit: visibility with responsibility, discourse with memory, and civic participation with intelligible structure.

## 19. Conclusion

CIVITAS Public is conceived as a realistic, visible and strategically sound entry point into a future European digital agora. It combines publication, debate, issue tracking, trend observation and democratic trust architecture in a form that is both usable in the near term and expandable in the long term.

Its promise is not perfection, but a credible beginning.

> **Readable for all. Trustworthy through verification. Valuable through accountability.**

Under the right institutional, editorial and technical conditions, CIVITAS Public could become the first concrete building block of a larger European democratic civic infrastructure.
