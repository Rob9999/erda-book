## Hauptrede

### Einstein-Zitat und Einleitung

*\[7:52\]*

In 1947, due to a particular concern of humanity regarding the recently developed atomic bomb, Albert Einstein said the famous phrase: *"I do not know what weapons will be used in World War III, but World War IV will be fought with sticks and stones."*

It is with this quote from this wise man that we'll start our dialogue — namely about what Albert Einstein did know back in 1947.

I believe I have a great opportunity to tell you **what weapons are already being used and will be used in World War III** — and what we should all do about it.

### Das transparente Schlachtfeld und die robotische Kill-Zone

*\[8:46\]*

Modern warfare — and even more so, future warfare — has long since gone beyond long trenches, powerful tank fists and air battles. The desire to survive, the rejection of the imposition of foreign will, exceptional creativity and freedom of thought have become an exceptional stimulus for Ukrainians to pursue technical progress.

And so modern warfare already integrates unmanned and robotic systems, distributed sensor networks and the entire electromagnetic spectrum — virtual, informational and partially physical — into a single theatre of operations. All of this is rapidly becoming intellectualized under the influence of artificial intelligence.

I have written many articles and delivered many speeches about the change of modern warfare — and even here I told about that. Nobody wanted to believe that, and many criticised me. Though this reality is behind us. It's not important who thinks what in the categories of peace and who stays in illusions of the past.

**Today's war between us and the Russian invaders is the way it is — and we know for sure that today there is no sufficient war power to prevent this war. Not a single country has it.**

Summing up the four years of full-scale war, I will stay briefly on main conclusions:

- **In modern war, the battlefield has become completely transparent.** This has led to the creation of a **robotic kill zone** which today extends at least **25 kilometres deep.**
- The ability to destroy logistics is constantly growing. Today this makes it impossible to use the so-called rear zone **up to 50 kilometres deep.**
- The classic logistical ruse that we successfully devised during the liberation of the Kherson region has gained new momentum.
- **All this has made it impossible to carry out classic offensive or defensive actions.**

### Menschen als knappste Ressource

*\[11:18\]*

The number of people who are physically capable of performing tasks in the combat zone is minimal. It continues to decline and is actually tending to **be replaced by robots.**

What about mobilisation or the possibility of improving it? The experience of both Russia and Ukraine shows that the **traditional approach to mobilisation in modern warfare has completely exhausted itself.**

1. **This war showed that, oddly enough, the most valuable resource in such a war is human resources** — because it takes a lot of time to restore them, which is much longer than, for example, production cycles for weapons. It is simply impossible to quickly replace such a resource on the battlefield.

2. Given that the battlefield has become transparent and is controlled automatically by robots, **a person's chances of survival no longer depend on the quality of their training** and lead to inevitable losses, which requires distancing people from this kill zone.

3. Our experience also confirms that the issue of mobilisation is a sensitive one and one that affects the **stability of society** in a war of attrition and its willingness to support such a war. That is why Russia does not declare mobilisation, which is sometimes used to scare us. For Russia, fighting with mercenaries and volunteers and fighting with forcibly mobilised troops are different wars. And the political consequences of such wars are different.

**Therefore, future wars will certainly not involve the mobilisation of millions of people. It is more about technological and economic mobilisation** as a guarantee of the continuous process of ensuring and maintaining technological superiority over the enemy.

Today robots not only perform auxiliary functions but also carry out separate assault operations and even take enemy soldiers prisoner. The use of any technology in these kill zones, as well as people, has become a real suicide mission.

### Demographie und Krieg

*\[13:44\]*

The Russian-Ukrainian war has taught countries with demographic challenges a critically important lesson. **The model of warfare that involves trading human lives for tactical gains is no longer a logical and viable option** — except for Russia. Also, any resource is exhausted for Russia as well.

On a modern high-tech battlefield saturated with precision weapon systems, this model is unacceptable not only on moral grounds but on those of tactical efficiency. **Paradoxically, as the technological sophistication of the battlefield increases, human resources become the scarcest — maybe the only fundamentally non-renewable resource of all.**

This is especially true for developed countries where demographic trends render traditional approaches to warfare less and less acceptable. **For example, on the European continent, there is not a single country with a positive birth rate.** So the loss of manpower on the battlefield would have huge socioeconomic — rather than mere military — implications.

Therefore, technological evolution towards the **robotisation of warfare** has offered an alternative that allows for the preservation of combat effectiveness while radically reducing human involvement and, as a result, human losses.

### Attritionswaffen vs. teure Präzisionswaffen

*\[15:27\]*

The ultra-expensive precision weapons that were a real gamechanger in the 20th century have been replaced by **weapons of attrition.** These are **cheap and mass-produced but highly accurate weapons** that deplete at a high rate expensive weapon systems — on the use of which, by the way, the NATO doctrine relies.

These weapons of attrition are being developed consistently and effectively. **No country can cope with such a challenge on its own,** especially if they choose to err on the side of caution. This calls into question the ability of even resource-rich countries to wage long wars against smaller ones.

Nor is it possible to build an effective and efficient defence using the old approaches, even if resources are available. For example, the war in Ukraine has shown that it makes little difference how many combat-ready brigades, corvettes, submarines, and even missile cruisers you have. This advantage can be nullified by a country whose navy was destroyed at the beginning of the war. **And let's be honest: who's really controlling the Black Sea now?**

Procurement of expensive and unique products would not guarantee security in the era of modern warfare.

### Wirtschaft als Lebensader des Krieges

*\[17:02\]*

A most important consideration: success in a high-intensity war of attrition with the deployment of cheaper and more efficient means of attack depends on the **resilience of the nation's economy** and its ability to ensure **uninterrupted power supplies.**

Simply put, it's about the ability to maintain military potential over time in the face of constant tensions, complex logistics and accelerated technological change. **It is the economy, combined with political and social preparedness, that becomes the lifeblood of war — and it is the main target of the belligerent parties.**

How can we make sure that the economy contributes to the victory? There are two stages of economic development in a country at war:

1. **The economy should be put on a war footing** — mobilised.
2. Then it should be **persistently fuelled.** This is the greatest challenge for Ukraine today.

What does it mean to mobilise or to put the economy on a war footing? It's simple: a series of measures should be taken so that **military-industrial planning becomes part and parcel of the planning of military operations** and does not depend on external supplies.

Do we see this happening in Russia? Yes, we do. There is every indication that the Russian economy has been put on a war footing as part of their public policy. We experience the consequences of this move every day and every night. **It is almost impossible to stop such a military steamroller. The only option is to destroy it.** That is why every war so far has inevitably led to an arms race.

I can assure you that **traditional defence-industrial models — centralised factories, long supply chains, rigid production cycles — are no longer fit for purpose.** Not only do they generate a lot of critical vulnerabilities, but they also fail to adjust timely to actual operational conditions.

### Energie als neue Front

*\[19:38\]*

The brutal war of attrition in Ukraine is a war against economic opportunities. So far, **the weakest link in the economy has been the energy sector.**

Ukraine's energy infrastructure has traditionally been built on a centralised model — large power plants and a single grid connecting the regions. This war has exposed the vulnerability of such a system to targeted attacks.

Over the four years of Russia's invasion of Ukraine, attacks on power plants and grids have become the main instrument of war. The aggressive goal has been to cut off power supply to cities. **By the end of 2024, up to 80% of the thermal power plant capacity in Ukraine was out of commission** and energy transmission to the population was halved. As a result, in the winter of 2026, millions of people have been left without heating and electricity.

Centralised facilities — large thermal power plants, hydro and even nuclear power plants, extended electricity grids — become **an Achilles' heel of national security** in times of war. If the enemy destroys several key nodes, entire regions are plunged into darkness.

Thus, **energy security is inextricably linked not only to the resilience of the wartime economy but also to national security.** Protecting energy facilities becomes as important as protecting territories.

### Nuklearwaffen

*\[21:28\]*

In my humble opinion — and I know it will be criticised — this super-powerful weapon is akin to a fig leaf on Atlas's body. It disguises the true capabilities of the one who possesses it. It only tells you there should be something behind it, but it does not reveal the real capabilities.

**It is first and foremost a weapon of self-destruction, not a weapon of deterrence.** Look at the Zaporizhzhia NPP, for example, which has become a hostage to economic and political pressure rather than a clear and present danger that it really is.

### Raketen

*\[22:23\]*

Missiles remain in active use despite their high cost of production. However, their function has transformed from a purely combat tool at the front line to a means of the so-called **missile terror** — striking civilian quarters and the state's critical infrastructure, destroying its economic potential.

This type of weapon is likely to remain the most expensive one in a war of attrition — not only because it is costly to manufacture, but also because at this stage there are no defence solutions other than anti-missile systems, which are even more expensive than the missiles themselves.

Should we have such missiles in sufficient quantities, the enemy will have to spend much more on expensive air defence systems, thus increasing the price of war for their economy. That is why some people seem to have concerns about Ukraine's having such weapons. Yet our access to this capability could be instrumental in our victory — as well as part of the forgotten **theory of non-nuclear deterrence.**

### Luftwaffe

*\[24:01\]*

The Russian aerospace forces are the second most powerful in the world, second only to those of the United States. In terms of the total number of combat aircraft, Russia has a huge advantage over Ukraine. However, Russian planes rarely venture deep into Ukrainian territory for fear of being shot down by Western-made air defence systems. Classic air battles are also rare in this war, mostly because of the pertinent costs.

For example, **a fourth-generation SU-35 fighter jet costs about $50 million and can be easily shot down by a $4.5 million Patriot missile.** This simple arithmetic shows that the aircraft could hardly be a weapon of attrition — but as a **platform for launching other weapons of attrition,** it is quite effective.

So today the combat planes serve as a platform for striking ground targets with weapons of attrition and as carriers of air defence systems, **never going within the range of the enemy's air defence.** One can state with confidence that manned aircraft have got their own kill zone and that the personnel are increasingly moving away from this zone.

### KI, Schwärme und autonome Systeme

*\[26:02\]*

I would like to reiterate that technical and technological progress has resulted in a profound transformation of all domains — sea, air, land, outer space, cyber — which continue to be rapidly transformed through the advance of AI.

This process has fundamentally changed the role of traditional weapon systems and has undoubtedly affected not only the nature of warfare but also the procedures for training, organisation and development of defence capabilities. All of this urges the military strategies, tactics and operational processes to adapt.

It is the emergence of new technological solutions that leads to the most revolutionary changes in the conduct of hostilities. In the past it was gunpowder, aircraft or nuclear weapons. **Today it's unmanned systems and AI.**

The Miltech revolution based on unmanned systems and AI provides unprecedented tools for war and destruction of the enemy's military and economic potential. The **key functions** that are constantly evolving, including through the introduction of AI, and that pose a real threat are:

- **(a) the swarming function**
- **(b) the autonomy function**

Simply put, in the near future, with the development of the resource base and hardware, we will see **smarter and cheaper drones attacking by the dozens or even hundreds** from different directions and altitudes — simultaneously from the air, ground, and water.

The autonomy of these drones will develop in **two main directions:**

1. **Centralised digital space** — a common system of real-time control and interaction where all actions and processes are still controlled by humans, albeit with the help of AI.
2. **Fully autonomous robotic complexes/swarms** — both in combat and support functions. Such drones will be endowed with a high level of individual AI that learns and develops in real time within the limits necessary to perform combat missions and **will not require constant communication** with each other or with command nodes.

This is, in fact, a **transfer of responsibility to a separate tactical system — a drone.**

Another option is a **combination** of these basic architectures, whereby the advantages of the network are used whenever possible and the system goes into **"autonomous darkness"** when there is no other choice.

Then logistically, the most pressing task for those who need long-term security guarantees is, among other things:

- **To work on improving swarm intelligence algorithms** on the one hand
- **To find ways to combat and counteract drone swarms** on the other

Naturally, the laboratory itself where all this is currently being tested is located in Ukraine.

> Let me remind you that on the night of the 6th to 7th of September 2025, Kyiv was attacked simultaneously by **862 Shahed-type kamikaze drones.**

So whether we agree or not: **the war of the future will be a war of autonomous and semi-autonomous robotic systems.** It is clear that the competition to use the full potential of autonomous swarm UAVs will likely provide a decisive advantage in both immediate combat effectiveness and security guarantees.

Of course, the prospects for developing drone swarm technologies — ground, surface and underwater — in Ukraine must be considered in light of the **critical dependence on global microelectronic supply chains.** Therefore, future **security alliances** will likely be built on the principle of **resource security.**

Unfortunately for Ukraine, the development of relevant national capabilities is impossible without the full implementation of a sustainable partnership and the introduction of appropriate policies in the field of military digital products and services.

### Übergangsperiode

*\[31:09\]*

From a technological point of view, the Russian-Ukrainian war is currently in a **transitional period.** This is a period when technology is developing rapidly but its development is still insufficient to, for example, completely eliminate human presence in the combat zone or ensure the complete autonomy of unmanned systems. However, it has been impossible to carry out the operational tasks of a classic war for more than two years.

Traditional weapons and structures are still being used but are gradually being phased out or transformed. With Russia unable to achieve its political goals in the war and Ukraine refusing to surrender, **Russia is stepping up its attacks on infrastructure, energy systems, transport hubs, and other key elements** of state administration and life support.

Therefore, **the strategy of the war is now aimed not so much at capturing territory as at depleting the enemy's resources and capabilities** in order to achieve its goals. All this is happening against the backdrop of the expected destabilisation of the old world order — also exacerbated by the US policy of non-compliance with the international legal framework, which would lead to the ultimate destruction of the existing international system.

Among other things, these steps by the US are exacerbating contradictions within NATO and leading to a decline in both the real effectiveness of the alliance and coordinated assistance to Ukraine. Russia meanwhile is exploiting the situation to reduce sanctions, expand its military-industrial complex, accumulate the necessary technology and obtain additional support from China — by **inviting Ukraine to negotiations.** It is starting a war on a much higher strategic level — **a war against women, children, and the elderly** — sparing neither expensive missiles nor Shaheds, forcing them to freeze in cold cities, expecting a quick surrender.

## Drei parallele Aufgaben

*\[33:36\]*

In such a situation, where the tension and intensity of the war of attrition is increasing, it is of course necessary to find a solution that would not only not harm the current geopolitical situation but will also become the basis for future peace — **peace based on the strength of potential opportunities, not obligations.**

Such a solution may include the implementation of **three parallel tasks:**

### 1. Partnerschaften bewahren

The first and most difficult task is to **preserve the maximum number of partners** and respect their interests, without which it is impossible today to withstand Russian attacks, exert pressure and effectively build a future defence alliance — without harming future allies.

This is a delicate and balanced diplomatic effort which has had no parallel in world history due to the completely unprecedented conditions in which all participants find themselves — because **the old rules no longer work,** but people still have high hopes for them, and the new ones have not yet even been formulated.

Personally, for me the main condition for this process is the **preservation of democracy and freedom and the standard of living** — which is the main achievement, for example, of Europe. At the same time, there's a great risk of coming to situational agreements, cynical arrangements and short-term interests without any common framework.

However, this must be done because it is the lessons of this war that speak of the need to join forces to create defence capabilities.

> **Individual states will be able to independently create technological superiority in certain areas, but no state will be able to ensure self-sufficiency across the entire spectrum of critical military technologies.**

Therefore, the search for alliances and partnerships for joint development and exchange of advanced technologies and strengthening of production capacities will become an absolute necessity — not only for us but also for countries seeking to ensure their own technological superiority.

**We will need technological alliances, not treaty articles** — where countries will combine their resources, technologies, and innovative potential with ours to achieve a common strategic advantage and, as a result, gain potential power.

### 2. Frontlinie stabilisieren und kritische Infrastruktur schützen

The second crucial task is to **stabilise the line of combat and protect critical facilities and infrastructure.** This is our homework.

When it becomes clear that this is a war of attrition where the main goal is economic survival, it is necessary to move away from the classic strategy of inflicting maximum damage and consistently defeating the enemy. In addition, probably due to the impossibility of improving mobilisation and demographic disparities with Russia, **the only strategy is not a symmetrical response** and following the enemy's logic **but rather changing the very nature of the war.**

We need to make the war more costly for Russia and, as a result, lead to its inevitable defeat. Then it is obvious that today the task of stabilising the line of contact is **only possible through maximum technological advancement of defence.**

But the transition to high-tech defence is only possible if there are **stable defensive positions.** If these positions are located on unfavourable lines and the front line is unstable, this can negate the advantages of technological superiority.

It is also important to understand that all this must necessarily be accompanied by the acquisition of the necessary **managerial discipline** which will provide the necessary advantage. That is why Russia constantly uses tactics of meat infiltration and control of rear areas — not only to capture additional territories but also to **prevent us from intercepting the technological initiative.**

The situation is the same with the protection of critical infrastructure. The problem is not even that more air defence and innovative solutions are needed to protect it, but that the system — for example, energy supply — can no longer exist and be defended in the form in which it was created in the 1950s.

In such conditions, it is probably necessary to pay more attention to the **strategy of decentralising energy** as a way to increase the country's resilience to any attacks. It is military action and its consequences that made this concept urgent. **Decentralisation went from being a question of efficiency to a question of national survival.**

Energy decentralisation involves a transition from a few large energy sources to many small ones distributed across the territory — the implementation of the concept of **distributed generation,** where electricity and heat are produced closer to the consumer at local low-capacity power plants, renewable power plants and micro- and mini-grids. It is precisely this architecture that will significantly increase the resilience of the energy system in the face of the threat of weapons of attrition.

This is an extremely complex process, but it is obvious and predictable — unlike promises not to destroy such a system or to produce more air defence systems. Presumably, the reconstruction process should begin now — without waiting for a peace deal which may never happen. And a new format of peacekeepers should be launched — **who do not fight, but rebuild.**

> **The main conclusion is that the energy system has become a new front in the war, and its stability determines the outcome of the conflict.**

The transition from the Soviet centralised network to a modern decentralised hybrid system is not only a modernisation but also **the country's ability to survive — and therefore to win.**

In any case, the preservation and protection of critical infrastructure must be based on **real rather than expected and uncontrollable capabilities.**

### 3. Sanktionen aufrechterhalten und verschärfen

The third task is to convince our partners of the need to **impose tangible sanctions** and not to ease the sanctions regime against Russia in a war of attrition. **This is the only effective mechanism for ending the war** — which consists in depriving the aggressor of the very possibility of waging war.

Russia's recent activity in the United States and the intensified terror on our cities testify to tangible problems in the Russian economy. This is a real signal coming from within the Russian state apparatus. The deterioration of the economic situation and the threat of citizens dying in war are gradually creating a fundamentally different situation in terms of Russia's perception of the ruling regime and calling into question the stability of the entire totalitarian regime.

It was precisely such an explosion of economic crisis that once forced the Soviet Union to end the war in Afghanistan and led to a phase of loss of control within the Soviet Union itself. The military economy, filled with huge expenditures on defence orders and payments to mercenaries, must be approaching a breaking point.

It is very important for us to **continue to strike at the sources of Russian income** in parallel with the financial support of our partners. Among the main measures should be a reduction in purchases of Russian energy resources and the **fight against the shadow fleet,** especially in the Baltic Sea.

Of course, Russia will have money for the war until the very end. However, it is important to understand that **restrictions on its economic capabilities and isolation must be irreversible** and the only thing that can lead to the end of the war.
